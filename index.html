About Me

I'm a PhD candidate with Southern Methodist University working on the ATLAS experiment at CERN. My research centers on searching for Higgs boson pair production (HH), a rare process that allows us to better understand the early Universe.

My work spans multiple areas of experimental particle physics:

  • Higgs boson pair production: Searching for di-Higgs events in multiple lepton, tau, and photon final states
  • Statistical analysis: Developing advanced techniques for signal extraction and systematic uncertainty treatment
  • Machine learning and AI: Applying neural networks to improve event selection and signal-background discrimination
  • Future collider studies: Projecting sensitivity for Higgs physics at the High-Luminosity LHC
  • Detector operations: Contributing to electron isolation performance and LAr detector shifts

Beyond research, I'm passionate about science communication and collaborative science. I've contributed to ATLAS public communications and meeting reports, and I believe strongly in making particle physics accessible and engaging to broader audiences.

When I'm not analyzing data, I like to spend my time hiking the Alps or having a drink at R1 with the CERN community.

Research Highlights

Higgs Boson Pair Production

The Higgs boson was discovered in 2012 at the Large Hadron Collider by the ATLAS and CMS collaborations. This discovery was considered to be the missing puzzle piece to confirm the validity of the Standard Model of particle physics.

Measuring how strongly the Higgs interacts with itself is a way we can probe some of the most important open questions in physics today. Measurements of the Higgs self-coupling can tell us:

  • Shape of the Higgs potential
  • Stability of the universe
  • Electroweak symmetry breaking
  • Baryogenesis
  • Beyond the Standard Model physics

My PhD research focuses on searching for non-resonant di-Higgs production in final states with multiple leptons, taus, and photons using 140 fb⁻¹ of proton-proton collision data at √s = 13 TeV. This challenging analysis requires sophisticated techniques to separate rare signal events from large backgrounds, including:

  • Multivariate analysis techniques using boosted decision trees and neural networks
  • Novel categorization strategies to maximize sensitivity across decay channels
  • Statistical combination with other ATLAS di-Higgs searches
  • Interpretation in both Standard Model and beyond-Standard-Model scenarios

HL-LHC Projections

I've led efforts to project the sensitivity of multilepton di-Higgs searches at the High-Luminosity LHC, which will collect 3000 fb⁻¹ of data. This work helps guide detector upgrade decisions and demonstrates ATLAS's discovery potential for Higgs pair production in the coming decade.
